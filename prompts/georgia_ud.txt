GEORGIA LEGAL REQUIREMENTS — DISPOSSESSORY PROCEEDINGS:
- Governing statutes: O.C.G.A. § 44-7-49 through § 44-7-59 (Dispossessory Proceedings)
- Court with jurisdiction: Magistrate Court in the county where the rental property is located (primary). May also be filed in Superior Court or State Court per § 44-7-50.
- Filing fee: Varies by county, typically $54–$150 for the Dispossessory Affidavit, plus separate service fees (typically $35 per named defendant for sheriff service). Landlord must verify the exact fee with the local court clerk.
- Who may file: The property owner, or the owner's agent, attorney at law, or attorney in fact.
- Prerequisite: The landlord MUST have made a demand for possession of the premises before filing the Dispossessory Affidavit. For nonpayment cases, the 3-day notice (§ 44-7-50(c)) satisfies this requirement. The affidavit must state that the demand was made and was refused or ignored.
- Sworn affidavit: The Dispossessory Affidavit must be sworn under oath before a judge, clerk, deputy clerk, or notary public.
- Court service: The court issues a Summons after reviewing the Affidavit. The Sheriff or Marshal serves the Summons and Affidavit on the tenant. The landlord does NOT serve these documents.
- Service methods used by Sheriff (O.C.G.A. § 44-7-51): (1) Personal service — delivering to the tenant directly; (2) Substituted service — delivering to any sui juris adult residing at the premises; (3) Tack and mail — if no adult is found, posting on the door and mailing by first-class mail to the tenant's last known address.
- Tenant's answer deadline: Tenant has SEVEN (7) calendar days from the date of actual service to file a written or oral answer with the court. If the 7th day falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or legal holiday, the deadline is extended to the next business day.
- Default judgment: If tenant does not answer within 7 days, the landlord may file an Application for Default Writ of Possession.
- Contested case: If tenant answers, the court schedules a hearing, typically within 10–30 days.
- Writ of Possession: Issued after judgment in landlord's favor. Typically effective 7 days after judgment. Only the Sheriff or Marshal may enforce the writ. Landlord must not attempt self-help eviction.
- Money judgment: Landlord may request a money judgment for unpaid rent, late fees, utilities, and court costs in addition to possession.

--- GEORGIA FILING INSTRUCTIONS — INCLUDE VERBATIM AT END OF PACKAGE ---
NEXT STEPS FOR FILING IN GEORGIA MAGISTRATE COURT:

Step 1: Locate the Magistrate Court for {{county}} County, Georgia. File in the county where the rental property is located.

Step 2: Complete and sign the Dispossessory Affidavit (Document 2) in front of a notary public, court clerk, or magistrate judge BEFORE bringing it to the courthouse. Many courts will also notarize on-site.

Step 3: Bring to the courthouse: (a) the signed, notarized Dispossessory Affidavit; (b) a copy of the lease agreement; (c) proof of the prior notice / demand for possession (Document 1 + your Affidavit of Service from Document 3); (d) proof of unpaid rent or violation (bank statements, payment history, written communications, photos, etc.).

Step 4: Pay the filing fee. Fees vary by county. Typical range: $54–$150 for the affidavit filing + $35 per named defendant for sheriff service. Ask the clerk for the exact amount.

Step 5: After filing, the court will issue a Summons. The court's Sheriff or Marshal will serve the Summons and your Affidavit on the tenant. You do NOT serve these documents yourself.

Step 6: The tenant has SEVEN (7) calendar days from service to file an answer. Check with the court clerk on the 8th day whether an answer was filed.

Step 7: If NO answer is filed: File an Application for Default Writ of Possession with the court on the 8th day or later. The court will schedule issuance of the Writ.

Step 8: If an answer IS filed: The court will schedule a hearing (typically within 10–30 days). Bring all documentation to the hearing. You will present your case before the magistrate.

Step 9: After a judgment in your favor, the court will issue a Writ of Possession (typically 7 days after judgment). Deliver the Writ to the County Sheriff's or Marshal's office. The Sheriff will physically execute the eviction if the tenant does not vacate voluntarily.

IMPORTANT: Do not change locks, shut off utilities, or remove any tenant property at any point before the Sheriff executes the Writ. Self-help eviction is illegal in Georgia (O.C.G.A. § 44-7-14.1) and may result in fines up to $500 and civil liability.
--- END FILING INSTRUCTIONS ---
